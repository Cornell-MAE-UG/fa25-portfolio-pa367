Problem Set 3 in Heat Transfer helped me move between scales and modes of heat‑transfer thinking by deriving a transient PDE from a control‑volume energy balance, practice steady‑state composite conduction with internal generation and sensor interpretation, and use thermal‑resistance networks for a practical engineering protection problem. Working on each problem strengthened my ability to write clear energy balances and translating physical terms into equations, know when to use transient PDEs, steady‑state ODEs, or lumped resistance networks, and check units, sign conventions, and the physical meaning of boundary conditions. For example, the assignment explicitly asks students to “Please write down the energy balance equation for this control volume” and to “Derive the governing differential equation that describes the temperature profile of the rod and simplify it as much as possible.”
The graded feedback on my submission made the assignment helpful. Small bookkeeping errors and missing boundary/initial conditions were highlighted, which taught me to be more rigorous about energy accounting and clearer about stated assumptions. The TBC/turbine problem in particular tied classroom theory to a real design tradeoff which I’ll apply when evaluating thermal protection strategies in electronics cooling, insulation design, or turbomachinery. This problem set improved my ability to translate a physical scenario into the right mathematical model and to interpret numerical results in an engineering context.
